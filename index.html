HE3004 Health Economics

Religiosity and Childhood Vaccination Rates in the United States

Analysed whether Christian religiosity was associated with state-level MMR, DTaP, and HepB vaccination rates using CDC vaccination data, Baylor Religion Survey responses, demographic controls, and a first-difference model. The project translated a public-health question into a measurable index, matched state-level datasets, and interpreted why a null finding still matters for vaccine policy.

Core finding No observable association

Across the three vaccine models, religiosity was not statistically significant at conventional 1%, 5%, or 10% levels.

Research scope U.S. state-level comparison

Focused on school-going child vaccination coverage and Christian religiosity across matched state observations.

Time frame 2014 to 2017

Compared two survey waves and vaccination reporting periods through first-differenced changes.

Outcome variables MMR, DTaP, HepB

Used vaccination coverage percentages for measles-mumps-rubella, diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis, and hepatitis B.

Controls Demographics and education

Included age, income, college education, and percentage White population to reduce omitted-variable bias.

Research question

Does higher Christian religiosity correlate with lower childhood vaccination rates for MMR, DTaP, and HepB across U.S. states? The report started from vaccine hesitancy literature and tested whether religious commitment explained coverage differences once demographics were controlled for.

Data and tools

Used CDC vaccination coverage, Baylor Religion Survey Waves IV and V, U.S. Census demographic controls, Annie E. Casey education data, and Stata 16. The dependent variables were state vaccination rates; the independent variable was a constructed religiosity index.

Model design

Built a state-level first-difference model comparing 2014 and 2017 to reduce time-invariant state differences such as culture, local norms, and baseline public-health systems. Controls included age, income, college education, and percentage White population.

Interpretation

The null result suggests vaccine hesitancy cannot be explained by religiosity alone in this sample. The report argues that vaccine-specific concerns, family stability, information access, and targeted public-health messaging may matter more than broad religious commitment.

Limitations

The matched first-difference sample was small, with roughly 30 to 32 observations in the final vaccine models. The Baylor survey responses may also contain social desirability bias, especially because respondents were reporting religious activity and belief.

What it demonstrates

This project shows data cleaning, index construction, applied regression thinking, null-result interpretation, and the ability to turn a policy question into a concise evidence story.
