Musings

Notes on AI and work.

AI model selection

4 Jul 2026

Different AI models should be matched to different use cases.

AI models are not interchangeable assistants. A fast, low-cost model is enough when the work is reversible: summarising notes, drafting rough copy, cleaning up text, or generating first-pass alternatives. The goal in those cases is momentum, not final judgment.

Stronger reasoning models become more useful when the work contains ambiguity, trade-offs, or hidden assumptions. Strategy questions, market-entry logic, operating-model choices, and stakeholder-sensitive recommendations need slower thinking because a fluent answer can still be directionally wrong. Coding agents sit in a different category: they are most valuable when they can inspect files, implement changes, run checks, and work through errors in context.

The practical skill is model selection. The choice should reflect risk, context size, cost, reversibility, and verification needs. The question is not which model is smartest in general, but which model is appropriate for this decision, this constraint, and this standard of proof.

AI trade-offs

6 Jul 2026

The hidden cost of AI is weaker thinking when thinking is outsourced too early.

AI removes friction, which is useful. The hidden cost is that some friction is where thinking develops. When every blank page is immediately handed to a model, output may arrive faster, but the uncomfortable stage of forming a position, noticing gaps, and building confidence in independent judgment can be skipped.

Attention, memory, and mental state deserve particular care. Overusing AI can make work feel easier while quietly reducing patience for hard reading, deep synthesis, or sitting with uncertainty. It can also create a dependency loop: if the model always starts the thought, the user slowly becomes an editor of other people's structure rather than the owner of the argument.

A useful pattern is to think first, then use AI: form an initial view, ask the model to challenge it, compare alternatives, and decide what still needs human judgment. Used this way, AI becomes a sparring partner and accelerator, not a substitute for attention.
