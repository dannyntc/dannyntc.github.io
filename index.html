HE3004 Health Economics

Religiosity and Childhood Vaccination Rates in the United States

Analysed whether U.S. states with stronger Christian religiosity saw lower childhood vaccination rates for MMR, DTaP, and HepB. The project converted survey responses into a religiosity index, matched state-level datasets, and tested whether changes in religiosity moved together with changes in vaccination coverage.

Plain-English answer No observable association

The data did not show reliable evidence that higher Christian religiosity led to lower vaccination rates in this state-level sample.

01

The question

Do more religious states have lower vaccination coverage?

02

The evidence

Matched state-level vaccination rates, religiosity scores, and demographic controls for 2014 and 2017.

03

The test

Compared each state against itself over time, then checked whether changes in religiosity predicted changes in vaccine coverage.

04

The conclusion

The model did not find a statistically meaningful relationship between religiosity and vaccination coverage.

Research question

Does higher Christian religiosity correlate with lower childhood vaccination rates for MMR, DTaP, and HepB across U.S. states? The report started from vaccine hesitancy literature and tested whether religious commitment explained coverage differences once demographics were controlled for.

Data and tools

Used CDC vaccination coverage, Baylor Religion Survey Waves IV and V, U.S. Census demographic controls, Annie E. Casey education data, and Stata 16. The dependent variables were state vaccination rates; the independent variable was a constructed religiosity index.

How the model works

The first-difference approach asks whether changes within the same state from 2014 to 2017 move together. This helps reduce the effect of stable state traits, such as long-running culture, local norms, or baseline public-health systems.

Controls and interpretation

The model also controlled for age, income, college education, and percentage White population. The null result suggests vaccine hesitancy cannot be explained by religiosity alone in this sample.

Limitations

The matched first-difference sample was small, with roughly 30 to 32 observations in the final vaccine models. The Baylor survey responses may also contain social desirability bias, especially because respondents were reporting religious activity and belief.

What it demonstrates

This project shows data cleaning, index construction, applied regression thinking, null-result interpretation, and the ability to turn a policy question into a concise evidence story.
